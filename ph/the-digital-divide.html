The
digital divide
— the gap between students who
lack access to technology
and those who don't —
is compounding equity problems
within Philippine schools.

In 2018, the World Economic Forum ranked the Philippines 53rd among 63 countries in terms of digital competitiveness. A report from the Department of Education (DepEd) further states that only 26% of public schools in the country have Internet access. There is also a lack of tech devices in many schools nationwide as computer laboratories are still being built.


The quality of tech in education in the Philippines is definitely a pressing concern, with education stakeholders repeatedly calling for capacity-building for teachers, allocating sufficient resources and partnering with the private sector to accelerate tech in education.


In spite of these limitations, Rising strives to equip high school students with digital competence.


We are an organization dedicated to promoting technology in the country for underserved high school students by bringing tech professionals together to actively provide digital opportunities to them, as well as create an inclusive digital competitive environment for them.

We believe that pairing technology with curricula allows students to gain technical knowledge and develop creativity, critical thinking, and problem-solving skills - essential attributes for the jobs of tomorrow.

The future is one where people with access to and knowledge of technology are able to reach their full potential. We strive to be the playmaker in closing the wide digital divide.


Our operations will start in the US. We will first provide services for underserved high school students in the US, and then reach out to others outside the country.


The digital divide exists in the US. According to surveys conducted by the ACT, 85% of underserved students (from low income, minority, and/or first generation families) in the US have access to only one digital device - most often a smartphone. American Indian/Alaskan, Hispanic/Latino, and African American students had the least access.

The digital divide is not only compounding equity problems in third-world and developing countries, but also within the US. The lack of access to technology impedes students' ability to complete homework and online assignments. This situation can only be expected to grow worse as teachers more routinely incorporate technology into their daily curricula.
