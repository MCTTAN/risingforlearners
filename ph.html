Millions of students in the US lack access to technology and high-speed Internet.
Rising hopes to change that by bridging the "digital divide".


Technology and the internet have become a vital part of modern living. Both crucially enable people to share information and ideas, and facilitate learning. Yet, billions of people around the world lack access to these tools.

In the US alone, 24 millino people don't have access to high-speed internet and more than 20% of households do not have a computer. People living in low-income households or in poverty are more likely to experience difficulties accessing technology.

This situation has created a digital divide in the educational attainment and income of those who can readily access technology and those who cannot. The digital divide also means that millions of students in the US struggle to realize their full potential.

In an increasingly digital world, that means these students are at risk of being left behind and losing out on valuable opportunities.


A survey finds 85% of underserved students in the US have access to only one digital device. For most of them, it's the smartphone.


- New research on students who took the ACT test, conducted by the ACT Center for Equity in Learning, found that 85% of underserved (low income, minority, or first generation in college) students had access to only one device at home, most often a smartphone.
- American Indian/Alaskan, Hispanic/Latino, and African American students had the least access. White and Asian students had the most.
- Nearly a quarter of students who reported that family income was <$36,000 a year had access to only a single device at home - a 19% gap compared to students whose family income was more than $100,000.
- The most commonly reported home internet connection was a monthly cellular data plan.
- Students from low-income families who rely on just a monthly cellular data plan reported that their home internet is higher quality, more reliable, and faster than the internet connection at school.


Education for Today's Future


Education systems have been resistant to change because education is so important - too important, some would argue, to experiment with. There is another way to look at this: in a rapidly changing world, education is too important to be left behind.

In the coming decades, the changes we are experiencing now - to politics, economics, climate, and technology - will become even more dramatic. 21st century education needs to not only prepare young people for jobs that didn't exist until the start of the century, but also in their personal lives and in the world.

Around the world, there is a growing global movement towards achieving the vision of 21st century education.


Technology in Blended Learning


The traditional classroom model does not adequately address individual students' strengths and weaknesses. Thus, many instructors today have begun to supplement their courses with online exercises and feedback, creating richer learning environments through multimedia. A 2017 Speak Up national report on K-12 student and parent findings found that students, parents, and educators are increasingly interested in leveraging emerging technologies to create more personalized learning environments where instruction is individualized to students' unique needs.

One of the main innovations of personalized learning is the adaption of blended learning into the classroom. Blended learning, or hybrid learning, is a formal education program in which students learn at least in part through online delivery of content and instruction, with some element of student control over time, place, path, and pace. Blended learning environments aim to combine attributes of online instruction, like efficiency, sufficiency, and freedom to access information anytime, with attributes of traditional classroom instruction, like enabling students to work with new information presented, as well as interact with peers and the teachers in the classroom.
